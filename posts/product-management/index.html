The Product Manager Role

    Is your product missing the mark? Don't just add more features!

    An excellent product is the foundation of a successfull company and this all hinges on the ability of the product manager.

    If your product is weak, everything gets harder and the company is at risk of failing. If your product is the best, everything gets easier and it becomes a force multiplier on success. Product management is a difficult and often misunderstood role. This article distills what I consider to be the core principles of the product management role.

    What is Product Management?

    Product management is the art, science, and practice of making successful products (or making existing products successful).[1]
    - Shreyas Doshi - Former Stripe, Twitter, Google, Yahoo

    When is a Product Manager Successful?

    A Product Manager (PM) is successful when your product solves the problem better than everyone else so customers choose you.[3]
    - Paraphrased from Marty Cagan - Founder of Sillicon Valley Product Group

    Product Manager Success Metrics

    An increase in the these metrics indicate that the PM is doing a good job:
    • User adoption
    • Business impact (i.e. revenue)
    • Customer satisfaction

    Attributes of a Good Product Manager

    The best PMs push through when faced with adverse conditions instead of blaming their circumstances. They also show the following characteristics:

    • Immense user empathy
    • Deep care/obsession
    • Low ego
    • Ownership Mindset
    • Influential communication

    Product Manager Responsibilities

    Before we dive into what a PM does, it helps to understand how the responsibilities are divided up between each role on the team. I like Marty Cagan's framework where each person is responsible for a different product "risk".

    Product Risk Person Responsible
    Valuable - do users want it? Product Manager
    Feasible - can we do it? Engineers
    Usable - can users figure it out? Designer
    Viable - does it make sense for the business? Product Manager

    A PM is responsible for ensuring a product is valuable (do users want it) and viable (does it make sense for the business). Here's how they go about that.

    What Does a Product Manager Do?

    1. Research

    PMs need expert-level knowledge in the following areas:

    • The product
    • The users
    • The data
    • The competitive landscape
    • Intricacies of the business


    The last bullet refers to how the product is marketed, sold, compliance issues, security issues, etc.

    A PM must become a world expert in these areas and that requires a lot of time and an ongoing effort. It needs to be a key priority in the daily life of a PM. When this is done well, the PM brings key knowledge to the product team that allows everyone to make better decisions.

    2. Prioritization

    Equiped with expert-level knowledge from their research, the PM's job is to get their team aligned on what to work on. This prioritization is a collaboration with engineering, design, and stakeholders, but the PM ultimately makes the final call.

    Prioritization should be informed by the PM's research. However, it also needs to be guided by a strong product vision and understanding of what will deliver real value.

    3. Solution Discovery

    PM's don't just walk away after handing off a prioritized list of problems to solve or outcomes to achieve. A good product manager is a creator that works closely with the team throughout the entire process. This includes driving the solution discovery process where the team discovers the best solution to solve their user's problem or achieve their business outcome.

    But the real world is complicated and solutions need to be tested to learn if they really work. If not, they need to evolve. This is where the magic happens, according to Steve Jobs in The Lost Interview:

    There's a tremendous amount of craftsmanship in between a great idea and a finished product...It never comes out like it starts.

    A PM leads this discovery process by doing things like:

    • Customer interviews
    • Prototyping
    • Usability testing

    4. Quality

    Finally, after all the discovery has been done, the learnings have been uncovered, the engineers and designers go off and deliver their solution. When they finished building and testing it, the PM is the last line of defense before it goes live. If something is not right, the PM is responsible for catching it and works with the team to correct it.

    Conclusion

    Ultimately, the foundation of a successful company lies in its product, and the product manager is the linchpin of that success. Setting that person up for success is essential for the future of the business.
