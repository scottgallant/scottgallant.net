    Is your product missing the mark? Don't just add more features!

    An excellent product is the foundation of a successfull company and this all hinges on the ability of the product manager.

    If your product is weak, everything gets harder and the company is at risk of failing. If your product is the best, everything gets easier and it becomes a force multiplier on success. Product management is a difficult and often misunderstood role. This article distills what I consider to be the core tenets of the product management role.

    What is Product Management?

    Product management is the art, science, and practice of making successful products (or making existing products successful).[1]
    - Shreyas Doshi - Former Stripe, Twitter, Google, Yahoo

    When is a Product Manager Successful?

    A Product Manager (PM) is successful when your product solves the problem better than everyone else so customers choose you.[3]
    - Paraphrased from Marty Cagan - Founder of Sillicon Valley Product Group

    Product Manager Success Metrics

    An increase in the these metrics indicate that the PM is doing a good job:
    • User adoption
    • Business impact (i.e. revenue)
    • Customer satisfaction

    Product Manager Responsibilities

    Within the broader product team, the group tasked with delivering a product, it's important to understand who's responsible for what. I like Marty Cagan's framework where each person is responsible for a different product "risk".

    Product Risk Person Responsible
    Valuable - do users want it? Product Manager
    Feasible - can we do it? Engineers
    Usable - can users figure it out? Designer
    Viable - does it make sense for the business? Product Manager

    A PM is responsible for ensuring a product is valuable (do users want it) and viable (does it make sense for the business). Here's how they go about that.

    1. Research

    PMs need expert-level knowledge in the following:

    • The product
    • The users
    • The data
    • The competitive landscape
    • Intricacies of the business


    The last bullet refers to how the product is marketed, sold, compliance issues, security issues, etc.

    A PM must become a world expert in these areas and that requires a lot of time and an ongoing effort. It needs to be a key priority in the daily life of a PM. When this is done well, the PM brings key knowledge to the product team that allows everyone to make better decisions.

    2. Problem Prioritization

    3. Solution Refinement

    This is the meat of the job.

    PM's don't just handoff priorities to the team and walk away. A good product manager is a creator that works closely with the team throughout the entire process. This includes driving the solution discovery process.

    Marty Cagan goes into more detail in this video (watch from 24:48 - 29:27).

    When your team comes up with a solution you need to test it with users as quickly and cheaply as possible. The PM must facilitate this process.

    4. Quality

    Finally, my personal preference is if PMs are the last line of defence for product quality. The first line should be the engineers solving the problems to create a tight feedback loop.

    Conclusion

    Ultimately, the foundation of a successful company lies in its product, and the product manager is the linchpin of that success. Setting that person up for success is essential for the future of the business.
