How we helped our 3-year-old overcome constipation

Our 3-year-old was terribly constipated resulting in pretty severe pain. This made it difficult for our family to function because she would just try to comfort herself and never wanted to leave the house.

Our doctor had prescribed Polyethylene glycol (PEG), a commonly used laxative powder that you mix in the patient's drinks each day. The brand we used was Restoralax. She had been on-and-off PEG for years and the problem was just getting worse. Our doctor explained there was likely a large mass of hard stool inside her body that she couldn't pass. In order to prevent it from getting worse, the laxative would liquify the new stool her body was producing so it could go around the large solid mass. The hard mass of stool never got passed and she had multiple liquid bowel movements every day.

This was all happening just as she was transitioning out of diapers and using the potty.

My wife started researching the long-term use of PEG and read many horror stories. We learned that PEG wasn't actually approved nor tested on children under the age of 17 even though it was commonly prescribed for kids of all ages. I was really surprised by this but the Restorolax label confirms this.

Our solution

My wife read this book written by a parent whose child suffered from debiliating constipation and decided that we had to change our strategy. https://www.amazon.ca/Doctor-Didnt-About-Childhood-Constipation/dp/1079267565

We followed many suggestions from the book but here are the key things that helped us overcome constipation:

  • Diet
  • Glycerine suppository
  • Switched from PEG to Magnesium citrate
  • Potty confidence

Diet

We made some small changes here.

Drink more water. We encouraged our daughter to drink more water, especially after eating dry foods (raisins, dried dates, etc).

Limit homemade sourdough bread. She was eating A LOT of this, and another family member said it resulted in constipation, so we just limited this as a precation. Not sure how big of a factor this was.

Tried to increase fiber For example, our daughter was into Sauerkraut (at the time), so she got that every day. We ate oatmeal for breakfast everyday to replace the bread. Our doctor said that bananas resulted in constipation, so we limited those.

Glycerine suppository

Our daughter was really reluctant to use a suppository. A doctor at the hospital used one with her and it helped her pass a large stool mass once. With a lot of convincing she let us do this. Since we didn't want to force her to do this, we spent a lot of time trying to convince her that this would help. And if I recall correctly, some days we lost the battle and some days she was cooperative. Ultimately, this helped her pass some very large hard stools that were blocking her up 2 or 3 times.

Switched from PEG to Magnesium Sulfate

We stopped giving her Restoralax (PEG) and started mixing powered magnesium citrate each day. We followed the does in the book linked to above, but you should talk to your doctor if you’re considering this. We continued giving her magnesium sulfate for a… few months ??? Potty Confidence

This was one of the most important factors, IMO. Our daughter was terrified of the potty because she associated it with painful poops. That held her back from having regular bowel movements. Ultimately, we wanted her to have regular potty time each day. I first started by trying to get her to sit on the potty for 10 seconds. I would count with my fingers and when we got to ten, she got off the potty and we would celebrate (possibly with a reward/treat). She was just building confidence without having a bowel movement. When her mom came in the room I would say "Guess what? She sat on the potty for 10 SECONDS!" in front of our daughter to help her feel proud. We eventually started doing 20 seconds, then 60 seconds, and so on. We would read books, play with toys, give her rewards, and eventually the potty wasn't scary anymore. She started sitting on the potty for 10 mins or so each morning where she would read books, eat some frozen fruit, and have a bowel movement. If she didn’t have a bowel movement in the morning, we often would try again later in the day.

Conclusion

Eventually, she overcame this problem but we had to keep on top of it.
